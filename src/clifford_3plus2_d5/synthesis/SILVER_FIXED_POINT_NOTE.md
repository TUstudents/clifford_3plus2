# Silver Fixed Point And BCC Scar Count

This note synthesizes an external research note about the origin of the silver
transfer root. It is not a completed sidecar theorem. Its value is that it
changes the question from "how does the lattice output an irrational?" to "what
integer coordination does the self-consistent boundary loop have?"

The reframing is valuable because lattices naturally force integers, not
irrationals. A self-consistency equation can then turn the integer into the
irrational.

## Metallic Fixed Point

Consider the fixed point

$$
x=\frac{1}{N+x}.
$$

The positive solution is

$$
x_N=\frac{\sqrt{N^2+4}-N}{2}.
$$

For the first few integers,

$$
x_1=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{2},
$$

$$
x_2=\sqrt2-1,
$$

$$
x_3=\frac{\sqrt{13}-3}{2},
$$

$$
x_4=\sqrt5-2.
$$

Thus the silver root is the $N=2$ metallic fixed point:

$$
\epsilon=x_2=\sqrt2-1.
$$

The hierarchy parameter used in the mass and CKM texture is usually

$$
\eta=\epsilon^2=3-2\sqrt2\simeq0.171573.
$$

This distinction matters. The fixed point produces $\epsilon\simeq0.4142$; the
mass/mixing small parameter is often $\eta\simeq0.1716$.

The later radial synthesis [PELL_RADIAL_THEOREM.md](PELL_RADIAL_THEOREM.md)
makes this precise: $\eta$ is the eigenchannel contraction ratio
$|\lambda_-/\lambda_+|$ of the silver Pell transfer matrix, while $\epsilon$ is
the one-step silver amplitude.

Certainty: `C:9` for the algebra. `C:7` for the notation distinction between
$\epsilon$ and $\eta$.

## Dyson Interpretation And A Sign Caveat

The conceptual claim is that $\epsilon$ is not generated by a one-pass
amplitude. It is a self-consistent return amplitude. In boundary language, a
closed recirculation should solve a Dyson/Feshbach fixed-point equation rather
than merely outputting

$$
V^\dagger(z-H_Q)^{-1}V
$$

once.

The continued fraction

$$
x=\frac{1}{N+x}
$$

is a valid self-consistency equation. But it is not identical, without
convention work, to the standard half-line Weyl equation

$$
m(z)=\frac{1}{z-m(z)}.
$$

In the existing boundary-response sidecar, the half-line formula gives

$$
m(2\sqrt2)=\sqrt2-1.
$$

The fixed-point note instead packages the same number as a metallic continued
fraction with integer $N=2$. To make this a theorem, the actual scar reduction
must derive the effective denominator $N+x$, including the sign, channel
weights, and spectral offset. Raw neighbor counting alone is not enough.

Certainty: `C:9` for the distinction between the two equations. `C:5` for the
fixed-point interpretation as the right research question. `C:3` until the
actual QCA scar reduction derives the effective equation.

## BCC Raw Coordination Count

The BCC nearest-neighbor directions are the eight body diagonals

$$
(\pm1,\pm1,\pm1).
$$

For coordinate boundary constraints, the raw surviving nearest-neighbor counts
are:

| Defect | Boundary condition | Surviving body-diagonal channels |
|---|---:|---:|
| bulk | none | $8$ |
| face | $x\ge0$ | $4$ |
| edge | $x\ge0,\ y\ge0$ | $2$ |
| corner | $x\ge0,\ y\ge0,\ z\ge0$ | $1$ |

Therefore a flat coordinate face does not force silver. If one identifies the
metallic $N$ with this raw channel count, the face gives

$$
N_{\rm raw}=4,
$$

and hence

$$
x_4=\sqrt5-2\simeq0.236068,
$$

not $\sqrt2-1$.

This kills the naive claim

$$
\hbox{flat BCC }(100)\hbox{ surface}\Rightarrow N=2.
$$

Certainty: `C:9` for the raw nearest-neighbor counts under the stated
coordinate half-space model. `C:1` for the flat-face silver claim.

## Why The Negative Count Is Useful

The negative count does not kill the fixed-point framing. It says that the
silver object, if present, cannot be a flat face with raw coordination four.
It must be a lower-dimensional scar or a weighted effective loop.

The natural codimension-two coordinate edge has raw count

$$
N_{\rm raw}=2,
$$

which is the right integer for silver:

$$
x_2=\sqrt2-1.
$$

This is suggestive because the flavor sidecars already use the language of a
scar/path defect rather than a broad surface. But it is not yet a derivation.
The effective metallic integer is generally

$$
N_{\rm eff}\sim \frac{\sum_i |t_i|^2}{\Delta},
$$

or, more invariantly, the coefficient appearing in the reduced scalar
self-consistency equation after the channel space has been diagonalized. The
QCA coin, boundary offset, missing-neighbor repair, and internal projection can
change $N_{\rm eff}$ relative to the raw count.

The concrete calculation in [BCC_SCAR_TRANSFER.md](BCC_SCAR_TRANSFER.md)
sharpens this warning. The scalar BCC edge quotient leaks:

$$
[n,n]\longrightarrow
2[n-1,n-1]+4[n-1,n+1]+2[n+1,n+1],
$$

and the bare BB Weyl coin has no nonzero spinor subspace that kills both
leakage channels. Therefore the edge count gives a necessary integer, not a
closed silver mechanism.

Certainty: `C:8` for the edge raw count. `C:3` for identifying it with the
physical silver fixed point.

## Research Target

The fixed-point note gives the next finite calculation:

$$
\boxed{
\text{construct the BCC boundary-scar return graph and derive }N_{\rm eff}.
}
$$

The calculation should be done for a small catalogue of natural defects:

$$
\hbox{face},\qquad
\hbox{edge},\qquad
\hbox{corner},\qquad
\hbox{body-diagonal scar},
\qquad
\hbox{path scar }u-a-b.
$$

For each defect, the output should be the reduced self-consistency equation,
not just a visual channel count:

$$

$$

or its normalized equivalent. Silver is derived only if this reduces, without
tuning, to

$$
x=\frac{1}{2+x}.
$$

If the natural scar gives $N_{\rm eff}=1,3,4,$ or another value, then the
silver-root origin is falsified for that scar. This is the right kind of
criterion: an integer from geometry, checked by counting and reduction, not an
irrational fitted by hand.

Certainty: `C:6` that this is a sharp falsifiable research target; `C:3` that
the BCC/QCA flavor scar will actually produce $N_{\rm eff}=2$.
